# ICE — Sales Playbook

## Purpose

This is the conversation guide for client acquisition. It covers:
- How to open a conversation
- How to qualify quickly
- How to run a demonstration
- How to handle the common objections
- How to close
- What to do immediately after a yes

This is not a script. It is a framework. The words should sound like
you, not like a sales pitch. The goal is a peer conversation between
two people who understand the NDIS sector — not a vendor pitching a product.

---

## The Fundamental Sales Position

You are not selling software. You are not selling marketing services.
You are selling consistency and intelligence — specifically the
ability to maintain a professional, compliant, sector-specific
content presence without the practice owner spending time on it.

The person you are talking to is probably an OT, a physio, or
a support coordinator. They went into that profession to help people.
Content marketing is something they know they should do and actively
resent having to think about. Your job is to make it disappear from
their mental load.

The pitch is: **"Let me take this off your plate entirely."**

## Where Conversations Start

### NDIS Provider Facebook Groups
Participate genuinely. Answer questions as a practitioner and
as a plan manager. Do not promote ICE in groups. Build recognition
first. When someone comments "how do you manage to post consistently",
that is the opening for a private message.

### LinkedIn (Personal Profile)
Post about NDIS sector content strategy from a practitioner perspective.
"Three things NDIS providers get wrong about social media" is a better
post than "I offer a content service." Attract inbound by being the
person who talks about this intelligently.

### Personal Network
OT colleagues, plan manager contacts, practice owners from professional
associations. This is the warmest channel. A direct message from a
colleague asking "are you interested in this?" has a conversion rate
that cold outreach never will.

### NDIS Yarns Page
The page itself is a lead generation asset. When the public proof
dashboard is live (Phase 2.6), link to it from the page's about section.
"Interested in how this page is managed? invegent.com"

### Referrals from Existing Clients
Built into the cross-promotion network. When NDIS Yarns features a
client practice, both pages grow. The featured client's colleagues
see their page getting professional content management and ask how.

## Qualifying a Prospect

Before spending time on a full conversation, you need to know three things:

**1. Do they have a Facebook page for their practice?**
If no: they are not ready. Offer to reconnect when they do.
Do not try to sell the content service to someone without a distribution channel.

**2. Are they currently paying for any content help?**
If yes: this is a competitive conversation. Ask what they are getting
for what they pay. ICE wins on vertical depth and compliance calibration.
If no: this is a new-spend conversation. ICE needs to justify the budget
allocation, not win a comparison.

**3. Do they have budget authority?**
For solo practitioners and small practices: they decide.
For larger practices: confirm they are the decision-maker before
investing in a full demonstration.

Five minutes of qualification saves hours of dead-end conversations.

## The Demonstration

**The best demonstration is showing them NDIS Yarns.**

Open the page with them on the call. Walk through three or four posts.
Point out:
- This is from an actual DSS policy update published 48 hours ago
- Notice there is no markdown, no bullet points — it reads like
  a practitioner wrote it, not a marketing team
- See how the disclaimer is woven in rather than appended
- See how it ends with a question to the audience

Then show the proof dashboard when it is live (Phase 2.6).
Numbers give it credibility. Posts per week, follower growth,
engagement rate. These are real outcomes from a real page.

**If they ask: "Will it sound like me?"**
The answer is: the first two weeks are a calibration period.
We build your brand profile before a single post goes live.
The profile captures how you speak, what you care about, what
you never say. By the end of week two, the content should be
indistinguishable from what you would write yourself if you had
the time.

## Handling Objections

### "We're not sure social media works for NDIS practices."

This is a ROI question, not an objection to ICE specifically.

Response: "Social media for NDIS providers isn't about going viral.
It's about being the practice that support coordinators think of when
they have a referral. That happens over 12 to 18 months of consistent
presence — not overnight. The question isn't whether it works;
it's whether you want to start the clock now or in another year."

If they push: share any engagement data from NDIS Yarns that shows
real interaction from the sector.

---

### "We want to keep our social media personal."

This is a fear that AI content will feel fake. It is a legitimate concern.

Response: "Completely understand. What we build is not generic AI content.
Before a single post goes live, we build your voice profile — who you are,
how you speak, what you care about, what you never say. The content
comes from real sector signals, not templates. Have a look at NDIS Yarns
and tell me if any of those posts feel fake to you."

Then show the page. The content speaks for itself.

---

### "$800 a month seems expensive."

Do not defend the price with cost. Defend it with value.

Response: "One new client referral from a support coordinator who
follows your page pays for three months of ICE. The question isn't
whether $800 is a lot — it's whether consistent sector presence
generates that one extra referral per quarter. Based on what we've
seen with practices that maintain an active presence, it does."

If they are genuinely at a budget constraint: offer the Starter tier
at $500/month. Do not drop the Standard price.

---

### "We don't have the bandwidth to manage another thing."

This is the strongest buying signal you will receive. They are
telling you their pain directly.

Response: "That's exactly why ICE exists. You don't manage it.
The content goes out automatically. You get an email once a month
telling you how it performed. The only time you need to do anything
is if we flag a draft that needs your eyes — and in a typical week,
that doesn't happen at all."

---

### "Can we trial it first?"

ICE does not offer free trials. The calibration period is 2 weeks,
but it runs on the first paid month.

Response: "The first month is the calibration period — we build your
profile, get the first posts live, and refine anything that isn't quite
right. At the end of 30 days you'll know exactly what ICE produces
for your practice. If it's not right, we'll refund that month.
But we don't do unpaid trials — the setup work is real and
it's not fair to ask for it without a commitment."

The 30-day refund offer is discretionary. Use it when you sense
a warm prospect who needs a risk reduction, not as a default position.

---

### "We need to think about it."

This is usually a polite no unless you ask a follow-up question.

Response: "Of course. What's the main thing you'd want to think through?"

That answer tells you what the real objection is. Address it there,
not in a follow-up email three days later.

## Closing

ICE does not use high-pressure closes. The right client will say yes
because the fit is obvious, not because they were pushed.

When the conversation has answered the key questions and objections:

"It sounds like this could be a good fit. The way to get started is:
I'll send you a brief onboarding form to get the key details about
your practice and voice — takes about 15 minutes. Once I have that,
I can have content live on your page within 10 business days.
Does that work for you?"

If yes: send the onboarding brief within 24 hours while the decision
is fresh. Speed at this point signals professionalism.

If they hesitate: ask what would need to be true for them to say yes.
That answer either resolves the sale or clarifies that this is not
the right time.

## After a Yes: The First 24 Hours

**Within 24 hours of a verbal yes:**

1. Send the service agreement (simple one-page document — does not need
   to be a contract lawyer's work, but needs to cover: scope, price,
   payment terms, cancellation notice, and IP of content produced).

2. Send the first invoice.

3. Send the onboarding brief (discovery questions for brand profile building —
   see 09_client_onboarding.md Step 3).

4. Confirm a 20-minute onboarding call for Day 3 to review
   the brief and ask follow-up questions.

Speed and process at this point sets the tone for the entire client relationship.
A disorganised first 24 hours creates doubt. A clean, fast, professional
start creates confidence.

## Tracking Prospects

Until a CRM is warranted (probably at 10+ active clients), maintain
a simple prospect list. For each prospect, record:

- Name and practice
- Date of first conversation
- Current status (conversation / proposal sent / decision pending / won / lost)
- Tier they are considering
- Key objection or concern
- Next action and date

Review the list weekly. Any prospect with no action for 3 weeks
either gets a follow-up or gets moved to 'lost' and off the list.
Do not carry indefinitely open prospects — they consume mental energy
without producing revenue.

## What Not to Do

**Do not lead with the technology.**
No prospect cares that ICE uses Claude Sonnet, Supabase, and
2,582 taxonomy topics. They care about their page looking good
without them doing anything. Lead with outcomes.

**Do not promise specific follower or engagement numbers.**
ICE produces the content. Distribution depends on platform dynamics,
boost budget, and community strategy. Promise consistency and quality.
Do not promise numbers you cannot guarantee.

**Do not take clients who do not have a Facebook page.**
The entire pipeline is built around publishing to social pages.
A prospect without a page is not a prospect. Come back to them
when they have set one up.

**Do not take on more clients than you can onboard properly.**
A poorly onboarded client churns in 60 days. A well-onboarded
client stays for years. Quality of onboarding is more important
than speed of growth. At current Phase 2 capacity, do not take
more than 2 new clients per month.

**Do not undersell to win a client on price.**
A client who is buying on price alone will churn when they find
something cheaper. ICE's value is in vertical depth and compliance
calibration — and those things cost what they cost. Sell the value
or do not sell at all.
